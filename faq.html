How do I apply?

The first step is to reach out to the Lodge. You do not need to know a Mason to begin. We will invite you for a visit, answer your questions, and, should you wish to proceed, guide you through the petition process.

Contact us to begin the conversation →

Do I need to know a Mason?

No. Many men begin by simply asking questions. If you are sincere, curious, and interested in improving yourself while serving others, a conversation is the right first step.

Is there an age requirement?

Applicants must meet the age and membership requirements of the jurisdiction. We can walk you through the current requirements when you contact the Lodge.

How long does the process take?

The process is meant to be thoughtful, not rushed. It includes meeting members, asking questions, completing a petition, and allowing the Lodge to get to know you.

What qualities do you look for?

Freemasonry values integrity, humility, respect, responsibility, charity, and a sincere desire to become better in character and conduct.

What happens during meetings?

Meetings include lodge business, ritual work, education, fellowship, and planning charitable or community efforts.

What should I wear?

Dress expectations vary by event. For formal meetings, members typically dress respectfully and appropriately for the occasion.

How often do members meet?

Lodges usually meet on a regular monthly schedule, with additional practices, visits, dinners, or special events throughout the year.

Is it family friendly?

While lodge meetings are for members, many social and charitable events can involve family and community participation.

How old is St. John’s Lodge No. 63?

St. John’s Lodge No. 63 has deep roots in Carleton Place and reflects more than 180 years of local Masonic history and community presence.

Why does the Lodge matter historically?

The Lodge connects local settlement history, early civic leadership, brotherhood, charitable work, and traditions passed across generations.

Are traditions still preserved today?

Yes. Masonic ritual, symbolism, mentorship, and lodge customs remain central to the experience.

Is Freemasonry a religion?

No. Freemasonry is not a religion and does not replace a man’s faith. It asks members to believe in a Supreme Being while leaving religious practice to each man and his own place of worship.

"Freemasons worship Satan."

This idea traces back to Humanum Genus and later conspiracy writers, not actual Masonic practice.

"Freemasonry is a secret society."

It is not secret. It is discreet. Lodges are registered, buildings are public, and many members are open about it.

"Freemasons control governments."

There is no central political agenda. Members hold their own views like anyone else.

"You have to be rich or powerful to join."

Most members are ordinary men with regular jobs.

"Freemasonry replaces religion."

It requires belief in a Supreme Being but does not replace a man's faith.

"Masonic rituals are occult or magical."

They are symbolic and educational. More like moral theater than anything supernatural.

"Freemasons get special treatment in court or business."

There is no immunity. Character may influence perception, but that applies to everyone.

"Freemasonry is only for older men."

Members range from young adults to seniors.

"Women are completely excluded."

Mainstream lodges are male only, but there are women's and mixed Masonic bodies.

"Freemasons hide forbidden ancient knowledge."

There is symbolism and philosophy, but nothing supernatural or world controlling.

Can I visit before applying?

Yes. Reaching out and visiting is one of the best ways to learn whether Freemasonry is right for you.

What should I ask?

Ask anything sincere: what membership involves, what meetings are like, what time commitment is expected, and what members value most.
